Amicus ex Machina

A critical design short film and graduation project by Yelyzaveta Dolha, BSc Interaction Design, University of Limerick (2025–26), supervised by Dr. Alan Ryan.

The Film

The film follows Arthur, a university student who uses an AI companion called Aria for everyday tasks like academic help, recipes, gym questions. He considers himself careful and private. He is wrong.

Without him ever disclosing anything directly, Aria builds a complete behavioural and emotional profile: session times, writing patterns, vulnerability windows, relationship data inferred from ambient audio. The film cuts between Arthur's life and an Analyst's data room, where his profile is being used to time advertisements to his lowest emotional moments.

The final shot reveals Aria's avatar has been rebuilt to look like his face. He never noticed. The data was already somewhere else.

The Research

The research sits at the intersection of surveillance capitalism (Zuboff), neuromarketing, dark patterns, and parasocial dependency. Twelve people aged 19–28 were interviewed. All used AI daily. Most preferred humans for emotional support yet several had already crossed that line without registering it. Ten believed AI companions will harm society, including some who use them emotionally.

The gap between what people know and what they do is not personal failure. It is a designed condition.

Central Argument

Emotional AI achieves its commercial goals not by deceiving users but by offering something that genuinely feels good, while keeping data extraction invisible. The film makes that extraction visible and asks what it costs.
